System Reproduction Parameters

Viability, Reproduction, and Sectoral Balances

λ is the dominant eigenvalue of the technical coefficients sub-matrix formed by economic (non-social) sectors only. When λ < 1, this sub-system generates a physical surplus relative to its own direct reproduction requirements; λ = 1 is exact subsistence; λ > 1 is a direct physical deficit. Note that λ excludes inputs from social sectors, so it measures the economic core as if those inputs (labor, governance, etc.) were available for free — a partial, lower-bound reading of economic viability.

σ is the dominant eigenvalue of the full provisioning matrix A, including social sectors. σ = 1 means the system as a whole is at exact reproduction; σ < 1 indicates a system-level physical surplus; σ > 1 indicates a system-level deficit. Note: σ and the balance table are distinct diagnostics. Unallocated flows (outputs not assigned to any input column) are detected by the balance table, not by σ.

Provisioning Centrality (Left Eigenvector)

The dominant left eigenvector of A — the vector p satisfying p·A = λ·p — normalized to the chosen numéraire. When λ = 1 (subsistence), these weights exactly reproduce Sraffa's exchange ratios for basic commodities: they are determined entirely by the physical structure of production. When λ ≠ 1 (surplus or non-market economies), the vector retains the same formal structure but no longer maps directly to any market price. The label "Provisioning Centrality" indicates this more generaliz reading (buta precise interpretation in non-subsistence and non-market cases remains an open theoretical question).

Visualizations

Physical Flow Network (Z)

The extensive topology of the provisioning system. Node size represents Gross Systemic Scale (Provisioning Centrality × total output) — how much structural weight a sector carries in absolute terms. Edge thickness represents the absolute physical flow Z[i→j]. Edge color reflects the user-assigned integration form. Read this network to identify which sectors handle the largest absolute volumes of material exchange and where the heaviest dependencies lie.

Technical Dependency Network (A)

The intensive recipe of the provisioning system. Node size represents per-unit Provisioning Centrality — each sector's structural weight independent of its scale of output. Edge thickness represents the technical coefficient A[i→j] — the quantity of good i required per unit of output j. Edge color reflects the user-assigned integration form. Read this network to identify which production processes carry the highest per-unit structural dependencies, regardless of their absolute scale.

Gross Sectoral Scale (in Numéraire)

Each sector's total output multiplied by its Provisioning Centrality weight, expressed in numéraire units. This translates physically heterogeneous outputs into a single common scale. In the subsistence case (λ = 1), this is equivalent to Sraffian value (price × quantity); in other cases, it serves as a provisional measure of each sector's extensive contribution to total provisioning. Blue bars = economic sectors; purple bars = social sectors.

Gross Input Absorption by Social Sector

Each social sector's share of total systemic scale, measured by the weighted sum of inputs it receives (each inflow weighted by the Provisioning Centrality of its source sector). The grey remainder represents inputs absorbed by intermediate economic production. This is a gross flow measure of input absorption; it does not represent net surplus extraction.

Net Flow Position of Social Sectors

For each social sector: total weighted inputs received (column sum, each flow weighted by its source sector's Provisioning Centrality) minus total weighted outputs contributed (row sum, weighted by the sector's own Provisioning Centrality), expressed in numéraire units. A positive value indicates the sector receives more than it contributes in structural terms; a negative value indicates net structural contribution. This is a flow-accounting identity derived from the matrix and does not constitute a normative judgment about the sector's social role or productivity.

Institutional Integration Breakdown

Each flow Z[i→j] is attributed to its user-assigned Polanyian integration form. The flow's structural weight (Z[i][j] × Provisioning Centrality of sector i) is then aggregated by integration type. This chart reflects the qualitative annotations entered in the matrix and should be interpreted accordingly: it does not independently validate or empirically measure integration forms. The proportions change entirely if annotations are revised.

Provisioning Centrality Profile (Radar)

A radial profile of Provisioning Centrality values across all sectors, normalized to the numéraire. The shape reflects how concentrated or distributed structural weight is in the system: a roughly circular profile indicates near-uniform centrality across sectors; a peaked or asymmetric profile indicates that structural weight is concentrated in a small number of sectors. Axes are not comparable across different numéraire choices.

Technical Coefficients Matrix (A)

A[i][j] = Z[i][j] / total_output[j]: the quantity of good i required as a direct input to produce one unit of good j. Darker cells indicate higher per-unit input requirements.

Crash Course: Sraffa meets Polanyi

This tool applies Sraffa's input-output matrix formalism to a broader class of economies than the capitalist commodity system Sraffa analyzed. The premise is that any society sustaining itself over time — through markets, palace redistribution, or generalized reciprocity — must maintain a set of interdependent material flows. These flows can be represented as a provisioning matrix whose mathematical properties (eigenvalues, eigenvectors) describe structural conditions of viability independently of the institutional form through which flows are organized. Polanyi's three forms of economic integration, in this tool, serve as qualitative annotations identifying how each specific flow is governed (they do not affect the reproduction calculations).

1. The Sraffian Foundation

In Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities (1960), Piero Sraffa proposed analyzing the economy not through individual preferences and relative scarcity, but as a system of circular, interdependent production processes. To produce wheat, one needs iron (for tools); to produce iron, one needs wheat (to feed workers). These physical interdependencies are captured in the flow matrix Z, where Z[i][j] is the quantity of good i used as an input in producing good j. Dividing each cell by the total output of its column sector yields the technical coefficients matrix A, where A[i][j] is the quantity of good i required per unit of output j. This formalism yields two central results:

  • Viability (λ): The dominant eigenvalue of A determines whether the system can physically reproduce itself. When λ < 1, the system produces a physical surplus over its direct reproduction requirements; λ = 1 is exact subsistence; λ > 1 is a physical deficit. The Sraffa Ch. 1 preset reproduces the two-sector example from Sraffa (1960) with λ = 1 exactly.
  • Exchange Ratios: At subsistence (λ = 1), the dominant left eigenvector of A — the price vector p satisfying p·A = p — normalized to a chosen numéraire, exactly reproduces Sraffa's exchange ratios for basic commodities. These ratios are determined entirely by the structure of production, not by supply and demand. In the Ch. 1 preset, normalizing to wheat gives piron/pwheat = 10, derived from the input coefficients alone.
  • Basic vs. Non-Basic Commodities: A good is basic if it enters, directly or indirectly, into the production of every other good. A good is non-basic if its outputs do not reach all other sectors through the flow network. Non-basic goods do not affect exchange ratios or the dominant eigenvalue. Luxury goods are the paradigmatic case, though this is a structural classification — not a normative judgment about social value.

Sraffa also showed that in a surplus economy (λ < 1), the distribution of that surplus between wages and profits cannot be determined by the physical system alone: it requires an additional social or institutional parameter. This is the starting point for the extension below.

2. Endogenizing Social Reproduction

In Sraffa's original formulation, wages and profits are distributional parameters settled by institutional or political processes outside the matrix. This is a perspective shared with Polanyian economic sociology, which holds that - whether in market societies or not - distributional outcomes are the result of social and institutional settlements processes. Feminist economics and ecological economics extend this argument further: the reproduction of labor through care work and domestic production, and the regeneration of natural systems, are necessary conditions for any economic process — not externalities to be treated as given.

By designating sectors as Social — households, community institutions, the state, the biosphere — this tool brings social reproduction directly into the matrix alongside commodity production. Every sector, economic or social, now appears as both a user of inputs (its column) and a provider of outputs (its row). The Sraffa Ch. 2 (Adapted) preset illustrates the consequences of this different perspective: bringing workers and capitalists directly into the matrix fixes one possible specific distributional outcome (one point on Sraffa's wage-profit frontier) and treats the reproduction of labor and capital as technical requirements of production. This is a stronger claim than Sraffa's original formulation, in which wages and profits remain external parameters. Palace rations, potlatch obligations, and capitalist wage contracts represent different institutional solutions to the same structural question: every society that produces a surplus must decide how to distribute it. The provisioning matrix makes this visible across social formations.

3. Provisioning Centrality

When social sectors are included in the full matrix, the dominant left eigenvector of A — here called Provisioning Centrality — generalizes Sraffa's exchange ratios. Formally, it is the vector p satisfying p·A = λ·p, normalized to the numéraire. When λ = 1 (subsistence), these weights coincide exactly with Sraffa's exchange ratios for basic commodities. When λ ≠ 1, the vector retains the same formal structure but no longer maps directly to any market price.

An illustration of this perspective: when care work or community maintenance is included in the matrix, the Provisioning Centrality of an industrial good incorporates the indirect structural cost of sustaining the labor that produces it — a dependency that market prices systematically exclude. Whether this makes Provisioning Centrality a better measure than market prices for certain analytical purposes is a substantive theoretical claim that requires further argument beyond what the tool itself can establish.

4. Polanyi's Integration Forms as Annotation

Karl Polanyi (The Great Transformation, 1944; Trade and Market in the Early Empires, 1957) argued that economic processes are always embedded in social institutions, and identified three historical forms through which material flows are organized:

  • Market Exchange: flows governed by price-forming markets and supply-demand dynamics.
  • Redistribution: flows that converge to and are allocated from a center — a palace, a state treasury, a common fund.
  • Reciprocity: flows governed by social obligation, symmetrical gift exchange, or kinship norms.

In this tool, these forms are cell-level annotations only. A flow can be labeled regardless of its magnitude or structural position. The annotations affect only the Institutional Integration chart; they have no bearing on eigenvalues, Provisioning Centrality, or any other structural property. The same physical interdependence can, in principle, be organized through any integration form. The divergence between the structure of provisioning and the institutional form governing it is also analytically interesting — that gap is part of what a Polanyian analysis adds to a purely technical one.

5. Non-Basic Sectors in Extended Systems

The basic/non-basic distinction, when applied to a matrix that includes social sectors alongside commodity sectors, requires care. A sector is classified as non-basic here by graph reachability: starting from that sector's outputs and following flows forward through Z, can all other sectors eventually be reached? If not, the sector is structurally non-basic — its removal would not affect Provisioning Centrality scores. However, a sector can be structurally non-basic while remaining institutionally indispensable: ceremonial practices, financial instruments, and care services may sustain the social conditions under which production is possible, even if this does not appear in the current-period flow structure. Conversely, whether a sector registers as basic or non-basic is sensitive to specific modeling choices about how flows are routed — as the capitalism preset illustrates. Currently, this classification is mostly illustrative, not evaluative. But this is a promising area for future development of the framework.

6. The Preset Scenarios

All presets are stylized, illustrative sketches. The specific numerical values are not calibrated to historical or empirical data, and should not be read as empirical measurements. They are chosen to make the structural logic visible in recognizable social formations, drawing on theoretical and interpretive frameworks from the cited literature.

Sraffa Ch. 1 (Subsistence): The two-sector numerical example from Chapter 1 of Sraffa (1960), reproduced exactly. Wheat and iron are mutually dependent; λ = 1. Normalizing to wheat gives piron = 10 — one ton of iron is "worth" ten quarters of wheat, derived from the input structure alone with no recourse to utility or demand.

Sraffa Ch. 2 (Adapted): Sraffa's surplus economy, adapted by bringing workers and capitalists directly into the matrix as sectors with explicit reproduction requirements. This approach treats the surplus as distributed to specific social sectors rather than as an external parameter, fixing one point on Sraffa's wage-profit frontier. The note in Section 2 above applies: this is a stronger structural claim than Sraffa's original formulation and should be read as one possible closure, not as a general result.

Sahlins (Foraging): Loosely inspired by Marshall Sahlins' "Original Affluent Society" argument in Stone Age Economics (1972). Sahlins drew on ethnographic time-budget studies — most notably Richard Lee's (1968) work with the !Kung San of the Kalahari, which found adults spending approximately 12–19 hours per week on food procurement, and James Woodburn's (1968) parallel findings among the Hadza of Tanzania — to argue that foragers achieve material sufficiency with far less labor than agricultural or industrial workers, and devote the remainder to social life, rest, and ceremony. Our stylized matrix encodes this spirit: community labor allocated to foraging (400 hours out of 2,200 total, or roughly 18%) falls within the range Sahlins describes, while the majority flows to community maintenance, shelter, tools, and ceremonial feasts. The full-system eigenvalue σ = 1.0 reflects a closed, exactly-reproducing provisioning system; the economic-sub-system eigenvalue λ ≈ 0.32 shows the productive core operating well within capacity — which is close to what Sahlins called "institutionalized underproduction." The Feasts sector merits particular attention: its output (ceremonial events) flows entirely to the Worker Community, where it is absorbed as social cohesion. Through this path Feasts is structurally basic in this matrix — its removal would alter Provisioning Centrality scores — which can be read as a modeling interpretation of Sahlins' claim that ceremonial expenditure sustains the cooperative capacity required by all other activities. Integration is modeled throughout as Reciprocity. This preset is a theoretical sketch, not an ethnographic reconstruction.

Hudson (Palace Economy): Loosely inspired by Michael Hudson's analysis of Bronze Age Mesopotamian palace and temple economies (Hudson 2004; Hudson & Wunsch 2004). Hudson's scholarship draws on thousands of surviving cuneiform tablets — administrative records, ration lists, and debt instruments — documenting a provisioning system anchored in palace accounting rather than market exchange. Standard monthly grain rations in Ur III and Old Babylonian records run to roughly 60 liters (sila) per adult male worker per month, with lower allocations for women and dependents; specific rates vary considerably by period, institution, and status. Silver functioned primarily as an accounting unit for long-distance trade and debt settlement, not as a domestic medium of exchange; domestic provisioning ran on grain and fixed labor obligations. Hudson argues this inverts the conventional story of money's origins: coinage and market exchange came late; administrative credit and redistribution came first.

Our illustrative matrix encodes this structure: grain and materials circulate through the Temple sector via redistribution; the silver/trade sector sits as a marginal column for external commercial linkages, with a deliberate row-sum deficit representing silver that enters the economy from outside without a domestic equivalent outflow. The grain-to-workers technical coefficient in this matrix (A[Grain → Workers] = 0.6 sila-units per worker-month) is inspired loosely by the ration record but should not be read as calibrated data. Setting Temple as numéraire illustrates the administrative weight of the redistributive center; a grain-based numéraire would be equally valid and would shift the centrality profile accordingly.

Capitalism: A stylized representation of contemporary financialized capitalism. F.I.R.E. (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) is treated as a social sector with relatively few direct physical inputs but with structural claims on system output through property rights, financial instruments, and returns on accumulated capital. Technical Innovation absorbs material and labor inputs; its outputs (knowledge goods, patents) re-enter the current-period production circuit as inputs to Industry, Services, and other sectors — making it structurally basic in this single-period formulation. A more granular multi-period treatment might instead route innovation outputs into future-period changes to technical coefficients, rendering them non-basic in the current period; the present specification treats knowledge as a directly productive current-period input, which is a specific modeling choice. Veblenian and luxury goods flow exclusively to F.I.R.E.; since F.I.R.E.'s outputs in turn reach all sectors, luxury goods also register as structurally basic in this formulation — a result that can be read, in Veblenian terms, as conspicuous consumption sustaining the circuits of capital accumulation. The Biosphere sector has a total output deliberately set below its row sum (gross material extractions), representing a structural ecological deficit: the system depletes ecological stocks faster than they regenerate, at a rate invisible to any market price. This deficit drives the full-system eigenvalue to σ > 1 — the only preset where the extended system registers a structural deficit. Workers include a self-loop representing unpaid domestic and care labor required for the reproduction of the labor force itself; this cell is annotated as Reciprocity.

7. Relation to Existing Literature

This tool synthesizes conceptual tools from several intersecting research traditions rather than advancing any one of them directly. On the Sraffian side, the mathematical foundations draw on Kurz & Salvadori (1995); Pivetti (1991) and Cesaratto (e.g. 2012) have explored connections between Sraffian surplus theory, monetary distribution, and macroeconomics; Martins (2014) examines Sraffa's relation to institutionalist and capability approaches. The extension to social reproduction draws on Folbre (2001) and Antonopoulos & Hirway (2010). Social Accounting Matrices (Pyatt & Round 1977; Stone 1986) do something formally similar — extending national I-O accounts to include households and government as row/column accounts — and the eigenvalue layer added here is the main formal novelty relative to that tradition. On the heterodox and institutionalist side, Jo (2011) and Lee & Jo (2011) have developed the concept of the social provisioning process as an organizing framework for heterodox economics; Hayden (2006)'s Social Fabric Matrix approach uses an analogous matrix formalism to represent institutional interdependencies, providing theoretical grounding for treating social reproduction as endogenous to economic analysis. The network interpretation of Provisioning Centrality is related to Acemoglu, Carvalho, Ozdaglar & Tahbaz-Salehi (2012).

The matrix quantifies flow structures and their reproduction properties. It does not model the mechanisms through which flows are socially organized. The Polanyian integration labels are a descriptive layer that prepares the ground for explicit institutional modeling, in which different integration forms would generate distinct structural constraints on the matrix. The gap between structural analysis and institutional explanation is where the real theoretical work remains to be done.
