Sraffa meets Polanyi: a Social Provisioning Sandbox

Load Model:

This sandbox represents the way a society provides for its needs as a matrix of interdependent material and social processes. Rows and columns below represent economic and social sectors. Read rows horizontally to follow how a good is distributed across sectors; read columns vertically to see what a sector requires to function. As presented by Piero Sraffa in Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities, sectors depend on each other in interconnected, mutually reinforcing ways (e.g. wheat farming needs iron tools, while iron smelting needs wheat to feed workers - these recursive requirements are what make this a provisioning system rather than a collection of isolated activities). The matrix can be seen as a snapshot of an economy in one period, with its parameters indicating whether the economy produces enough materials to replenish itself and reproduce itself into the future.

Select a preset economy above and press Calculate System Parameters to see the structural properties of the system. Then experiment: you can input numbers directly into a cell (inter-sector flow) or in the TOTAL OUTPUT box, add or remove sectors, toggle designations, or right-click cells. Click the calculate button again to see the effect of your changes.

System Reproduction Parameters

Viability and Reproduction (Eigenvalues and Sector Balances)

The core subsystem eigenvalue (λ) measures the isolated viability of the open economic matrix, approximating Sraffa's original commodity framework. The extended system eigenvalue (σ) measures the fully closed provisioning matrix, including social sectors.

λ is the dominant eigenvalue of the technical coefficients sub-matrix formed by economic (non-social) sectors only. When λ < 1, this sub-system generates a physical surplus relative to its own direct reproduction requirements; λ = 1 is exact subsistence; λ > 1 is a direct physical deficit. Because λ excludes inputs from social sectors, it measures the economic core as if those inputs (labor, governance, ecological services) were freely available — a partial, lower-bound reading of economic viability.

σ is the dominant eigenvalue of the full provisioning matrix A, including social sectors. σ = 1 means the system as a whole is at exact reproduction; σ < 1 indicates a system-level physical surplus; σ > 1 indicates a system-level deficit. σ and the balance table are distinct diagnostics: σ measures the reproductive condition implied by the technical coefficients; the balance table detects unallocated flows (row sums differing from stated total outputs). This implementation interprets "social reproduction" in the broadest possible sense of "the society subsists in time until the next period".

Provisioning Centrality (Left Eigenvector)

These weights (normalized to the numéraire) act as a system-level indicator of the relational dependencies between all the sectors included in the matrix. A sector's provisioning centrality weight indicates the structural importance of this sector in the provisioning network, i.e., how much of the flows from. In a strictly economic, closed system at subsistence (as in Sraffa's Chapter 1), these also correspond to market prices.

Physical UnitWeights

The dominant left eigenvector of A — the vector p satisfying p·A = λ·p — normalized to the chosen numéraire. In Sraffa's narrowly economic framework at exact subsistence (before surplus distribution is introduced), these weights correspond to market prices. In our extended framework, regardless of the institutional form of integration, the vector provides a systemic measure of how much of the provisioning network required to sustain each sector. These are (in some sense to be explored) analogues to prices in market systems.

Physical Flow Network (Z)

Visualizes the absolute material activity in the system. Larger nodes represent greater systemic scale; thicker edges indicate higher physical flow volumes.

The "extensive" topology: node size represents Gross Systemic Scale (Provisioning Centrality × total output), combining structural importance with output volume. Edge thickness represents the absolute physical flow Z[i→j]. Edge color reflects the user-assigned integration form. This network shows where the largest absolute volumes of material exchange occur in the system — which sectors are big in aggregate terms, and where the heaviest flow dependencies lie. Node colors map to economic (blue) and social (violet) classifications.

Technical Dependency Network (A)

Visualizes the per-unit technical dependencies. Larger nodes indicate higher systemic centrality per unit of output; thicker edges represent larger input requirements per unit produced.

The "intensive" structure: node size represents per-unit Provisioning Centrality — structural importance independent of output scale. Edge thickness represents the technical coefficient A[i→j]: the quantity of good i required per unit of output j. Edge color reflects the user-assigned integration form. This network shows which production processes carry the highest per-unit structural dependencies — the recipe of the economy stripped of its scale.

Gross Sectoral Scale (in Numéraire)

Calculates the total size of each sector within the provisioning system.

Each sector's total output multiplied by its Provisioning Centrality weight, expressed in numéraire units. This translates physically heterogeneous outputs (tons of wheat, hours of labor, ecological services) into a single common structural scale. In the subsistence case (λ = 1), this equals Sraffian value (price × quantity). In other cases, it is a provisional measure of each sector's extensive contribution to total provisioning. Blue bars = economic sectors; violet bars = social sectors.

Gross Input Absorption by Social Sector

Displays the share of total systemic activity flowing through each social sector as inputs received.

Each social sector's share of total systemic scale, measured by the weighted sum of inputs it receives (each inflow weighted by the Provisioning Centrality of its source sector). The grey remainder represents inputs absorbed by intermediate economic production. This is a gross flow measure of input absorption; it does not represent net surplus extraction.

Net Flow Position of Social Sectors

Calculates whether social sectors send more outflows into the system than they receive (as measured by respective system weights), or vice versa.

For each social sector: total weighted inputs received (column sum, each flow weighted by its source sector's Provisioning Centrality) minus total weighted outputs contributed (row sum, weighted by the sector's own Provisioning Centrality), expressed in numéraire units. A positive value indicates the sector receives more than it contributes structurally; negative indicates net structural contribution. Note that inputs and outputs use different weights (source-sector centrality vs. the sector's own centrality), creating an asymmetry that should be interpreted with care. This is a flow-accounting identity derived from the matrix structure; it does not constitute a normative judgment about the sector's social role or productivity.

Institutional Integration Breakdown

Aggregates total systemic scale according to the user-assigned integration forms.

Each flow Z[i→j] is attributed to its user-assigned Polanyian integration form. The flow's structural weight (Z[i][j] × Provisioning Centrality of sector i) is aggregated by integration type. This chart reflects the qualitative annotations entered in the matrix and should be interpreted accordingly — it does not independently validate or empirically measure integration forms. The proportions change entirely if annotations are revised.

Sraffa and Polanyi: A Conceptual Introduction to the Social Provisioning Framework

This sandbox applies Sraffa's input-output matrix formalism to a broader class of economies than the capitalist commodity system Sraffa analyzed. The premise is that any society sustaining itself over time — through markets, palace redistribution, or generalized reciprocity — must maintain a set of interdependent material flows. These flows can be represented as a provisioning matrix whose mathematical properties (eigenvalues, eigenvectors) describe structural conditions of viability independently of the institutional form through which they are organized.

This tool is intended as an intuition pump: a thought-experiment device for making ideas about provisioning systems tangible and manipulable (in other words, this is not a tool for actual empirical research). The presets are only illustrations meant to convey the spirit of the cases, the institutional annotations are impressionistic and not formally connected to the rest of the mathematical framework, and the mathematics and visualizations rely on non-rigorous HTML-embedded calculations. The last section of the document sketches how this might be developed towards actual research applications.

1. Sraffa and Material Reproduction

In Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities (1960), Piero Sraffa proposed analyzing the economy not through the lens of individual subjective preferences, utility maximization, and the relative scarcity of given resources. Instead, he conceptualized the economy as an objective system of circular, interdependent production processes, where commodities are produced by means of other commodities. To produce wheat, one needs iron (for tools); to produce iron, one needs wheat (to feed workers). These physical interdependencies are captured in the flow matrix Z, where Z[i][j] is the quantity of good i used as an input in producing good j. Dividing each cell by the total output of its column sector yields the technical coefficients matrix A, where A[i][j] is the quantity of good i required per unit of output j. This formalism yields two results:

  • Viability (λ): The dominant eigenvalue of A determines whether the system can physically reproduce itself. When λ < 1, the system produces a physical surplus over its direct reproduction requirements; λ = 1 is exact subsistence; λ > 1 is a physical deficit. The Sraffa Ch. 1 preset reproduces the two-sector numerical example from Sraffa with λ = 1 exactly. Normalizing to wheat gives piron/pwheat = 10.
  • Exchange Ratios: At subsistence (λ = 1), the dominant left eigenvector of A (the price vector p satisfying p·A = p), normalized to a chosen numéraire, exactly reproduces Sraffa's exchange ratios for basic commodities.
  • Basic vs. Non-Basic Commodities: A process is basic if its outputs enter, directly or indirectly, into the reproduction of every other process in the system. A process is non-basic if its outputs cannot reach all other processes through the flow network — meaning the system could reproduce without them (in formal terms, their removal does not alter the dominant eigenvalue or exchange ratios of the basic sub-system).

Sraffa also showed that in a surplus economy (λ < 1), the distribution of the surplus between wages and profits cannot be determined by the physical system alone: it requires an additional social or institutional parameter. This is the starting point for the extension below.

2. Endogenizing Social Reproduction

In Sraffa's original formulation, wages and profits are distributional parameters settled by institutional or political processes outside the matrix. This is very much in agreement with the perspective of embeddedness in Polanyian economic sociology, which holds that distributional outcomes are the result of social and institutional settlements rather than outcomes of the market. In fact, this is also true for non-market economies, which must also reproduce themselves through institutional processes. Feminist and ecological economics extend this further: the reproduction of labor through care work and domestic production, and the regeneration of natural systems, are necessary conditions for any economic process (not externalities to be treated as given), and these broader physical and social processes make a difference to the functioning of markets. By designating sectors as Social and incuding them in the same matrix, we bring institutions and reproduction into the same formalism that handles commodity production. Every sector, economic or social, now appears as both a user of inputs (its column) and a provider of outputs (its row).

When social sectors are included in the full matrix, the dominant left eigenvector of A (here called Provisioning Centrality) can be seen as an extended, more general version of Sraffa's price vectors, now no longer restricted to market societies. In Sraffa's subsistence model, this eigenvector determines the exchange ratios required for the economic core to exactly reproduce itself. In our extended model, it calculates the proportion of a system's total reproductive output required to sustain any given sector, whether economic or social.

In Sraffa's Chapter 2, distributing a physical surplus as a uniform rate of profit requires homogenizing heterogeneous goods into a common unit of account. Because our version of the matrix maps material flows without reference to monetary values, distributing a physical surplus to a social sector yields structural weights that diverge from Sraffian prices. This divergence is connected to how specific institutional conventions (like monetary value accounting) represent specific ways to implement a broader process of economic provisioning. Palace rations, potlatch obligations, and capitalist wage contracts represent different institutional solutions to the same structural issue - the fact that every society that produces a surplus must organize its distribution.

This extension requires some care with interpreting the basic/non-basic distinction. The tool detects non-basic processes by "graph reachability": starting from a sector's outputs and following flows forward through Z, can all other sectors eventually be reached? If not, the sector is structurally non-basic — its outputs are absorbed without feeding back into the full reproduction circuit. However, in the extended provisioning matrix, many activities that look non-basic in commodity terms turn out to be basic in the broader substantivist sense. In the Sahlins preset, the Feasts sector absorbs biomass and community labor but feeds its output entirely to the Worker Community, which supplies labor to all productive activities. Feasts is therefore structurally basic: it sustains the social cohesion on which all other processes depend. A sector is genuinely non-basic in the extended matrix only when its outputs reach no other process. An example is the Waste sector in the Custom preset: it is produced as a byproduct but feeds back into nothing. Defining what constitutes necessary reproduction in a substantivist framework is a relevant problem requiring further reflection.

3. Polanyi's Forms of Integration

Karl Polanyi drew a fundamental distinction between the formal meaning of economy ('rational allocation of scarce means', in the classic formulation of what economics does) and the substantive meaning: the actual material provisioning of human societies through people's relationships with nature and with each other. For Polanyi, the formal definition is historically specific to market economies, but the substantive definition applies to all human societies. The economy, in the substantive sense, is an "instituted process": material provisioning is always organized through social institutions — whether kinship systems, temples, states, or markets — and cannot be understood independently of them.

Cell background colors in the matrix annotate the primary form of integration through which a flow is organized. Polanyi identified three main historically recurring forms through which material flows can be institutionally organized, each underpinned by a distinct structural principle:

  • Market Exchange rests on the principle of a price-forming market system: flows are mediated by supply, demand, and the competitive formation of prices. Exchange is impersonal, bilateral, and motivated by price differentials. Polanyi argued that genuine price-forming markets are historically rare and institutionally fragile — they require extensive political and legal infrastructure to maintain and tend to disembed from the social relations that previously governed them.
  • Redistribution rests on the principle of centricity: flows converge to a center (a palace, a state treasury, a religious institution, a common fund) and are reallocated outward from it. The center may be a physical location, a social institution, or a rule system. Redistribution requires a stable political authority capable of collecting, storing, and allocating resources according to explicit or customary rules.
  • Reciprocity rests on the principle of symmetry: flows pass between symmetrically positioned social groups — moieties, households, kin groups — bound by mutual obligation. Reciprocity does not require a center or a price; it requires social relationships of recognized equivalence and obligation. Gift economies, potlatch obligations, and household sharing largely operate on the reciprocity principle.

Polanyi also drew attention to what he called fictitious commodities. Labor, land, and money are not produced for sale (they arise from social life, nature, and political authority), yet market economies treat them as if they were ordinary commodities, subjecting them to market dynamics such as supply and demand. Including elements like labor, other social sectors, and natural resources in the matrix brings this to the fore: they appear as a key part of the socioeconomic provisioning process, but their reproduction logic differs from the logic of typical commodities.

In this tool, the three integration forms serve as cell-level annotations only (they do not alter eigenvalues, Provisioning Centrality, or any other property of the system, and only affect the aesthetics of the visualizations). However, they point at an underlying premise about the embeddedness of the economy in society: the same set of physical interdependences that reproduce a given economy can, at least in principle, be organized through different forms of integration. This tool only points towards this, but relationships between specific processes of provisioning and the institutional forms that govern them is an important analytical question that a Sraffa-Polanyi synthesis is very well positioned to illuminate.

4. The Preset Scenarios

The tool includes 5 preset matrixes capturing the logic of social provisioning in different social formations. These are stylized, illustrative sketches — the specific numerical values are not calibrated to historical, ethnographic, or empirical data, and should not be understood as empirical measurements. The goal here is only to capture some of the likely forms taken by provisioning in these well-studied systems, using our own formalism.

Sraffa Ch. 1 (Subsistence): The two-sector numerical example from Sraffa's Chapter 1, reproduced exactly. Wheat and iron are mutually dependent; λ = 1. The exchange ratio piron/pwheat = 10 emerges from the input structure alone.

Sraffa Ch. 2 (Adapted): Sraffa's surplus economy, adapted by bringing workers and capitalists directly into the matrix as sectors with explicit reproduction requirements. In this preset, λ ≈ 0.647 for the economic core (wheat and iron alone), confirming an economic surplus of material goods. σ = 1.00 for the full provisioning system is a mathematical consequence of the matrix being fully closed: every unit of every good is absorbed within the system as an input somewhere (all row sums equal total outputs). In this specific preset, we modeled a political settlement where the 280 units of original agricultural grain input are split between seed (80), worker biological subsistence (160), and capitalist baseline consumption (40). The remaining 175 units of surplus wheat are distributed roughly 70% to Capitalists (122.5 units) and 30% to Workers (52.5 units) as discretionary consumption. This specific allocation represents only one point on Sraffa's wage-profit curve. It is a choice we made to illustrate Sraffa's Chapter 2 within our framework, pre-calibrating it to σ = 1. The broader theoretical implications of closing the matrix in different ways require further discussion and theory.

Sahlins (Foraging): Inspired by Marshall Sahlins' "Original Affluent Society" argument. Sahlins drew on ethnographic time-budget studies — most notably Richard Lee's work with the !Kung San and James Woodburn's findings among the Hadza — to argue that foragers achieve material sufficiency with far less labor than industrial workers (adults would roughly 12–19 hours per week on food procurement, devoting the remainder of their time to social life, rest, and ceremony). In this spirit, we allocated 400 hours (out of 2,200 total) of community labor to the Foraging sector; the rest flows to community maintenance, temporary camps, tools, and ceremonial feasts. The Mobile Shelter sector indicates a nomadic life, with frequent changes in settlement location (and little accumulation of physical property). λ ≈ 0.32 for the economic sub-system indicates the productive core operates well within capacity. σ = 1.0 reflects a closed, exactly-reproducing provisioning system where potential physical surpluses are absorbed by social expenditure. The Feasts sector is structurally basic in this matrix, illustrating our substantivist interpretation - that is, social expenditure can be seen as a production input in a more general, extended sense. Integration is modeled throughout as Reciprocity.

Hudson (Palace Economy): Loosely based on Michael Hudson's analysis of Bronze Age Mesopotamian palace and temple economies. Hudson's scholarship draws on cuneiform tablets documenting a provisioning system anchored in centralized temple-palace accounting rather than market exchange. Standard monthly grain rations in Ur III and Old Babylonian records run to roughly 60 liters (sila) per adult male worker per month, with lower allocations for women and dependents. Domestic provisioning ran on grain and fixed labor obligations, while silver functioned primarily as an accounting unit for long-distance trade. The administration pegged 1 shekel of silver to 1 gur (300 sila) of grain (in our preset, we make the input requirements for the Silver sector proportional to those of the Grain sector according to this ration, so that the eigenvector calculation results in an identical Provisioning Centrality weights). Modeled in this way, the economic surplus (λ ≈ 0.17) generated by the agricultural core reflects the temple complex extracting output to feed dependent labor (corvée, weavers) and trade for external luxuries. While not represented in this static matrix capturing only physical reproduction, Hudson notes that agrarian debts grew exponentially and were periodically cleared by "Clean Slate" debt cancellations. A post-cancellation period could be represented in the matrix by a different distribution of grain between workers and the temple.

Capitalism: A stylized representation of contemporary financialized, but innovative, capitalism. This preset is a loose collage of several different themes relevant to economics and sociology. F.I.R.E. (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) is treated as a social sector with relatively few direct physical inputs but with claims on system output through property rights and financial instruments. Technical Innovation absorbs material and labor inputs; its outputs (knowledge goods, patents) re-enter the production circuit as inputs to Industry, Services, and other sectors. Veblenian/Luxury goods flow exclusively to F.I.R.E.. The Work sector features a large self-loop (Z[4][4] = 6,900 hours, annotated as Reciprocity) representing unpaid domestic and care labor (the work assigned to other sectors is understood as paid; this reflects estimates for the care-to-paid-work ratio in advanced economies). The Biosphere sector has a total output set below its row sum (gross material extractions), indicating rapid depletion of ecological stocks. Waste is modeled as a non-basic terminal sink (output flows nowhere). The economic core is at surplus because the core isolates Industry and Services, treating their dependencies on Work and Biosphere as if they were free. However, note that the full-system eigenvalue approximates 1, indicating a that the complete system is able to reproduce itself (at least while ecological stocks are not depleted).

5. Related Literature, Research Directions

The matrix formalism, preset scenarios, and conceptual framing in this sandbox loosely implement ideas developed across several current research traditions. Sraffa's framework has been developed in an extensive neo-Ricardian literature (e.g., Kurz and Salvadori; Pivetti for monetary and distributional extensions; Martins for institutionalist and capability approaches; Chiodi for non-self-replacing states). The theoretical case for combining the classical surplus approach with Polanyian institutional analysis is central to the ongoing work of Cesaratto and Di Bucchianico. The treatment of social reproduction as endogenous to economic analysis is grounded in the social provisioning framework (Jo, Lee) and the Social Fabric Matrix (Hayden), alongside extensions in Social Accounting Matrices (Pyatt, Round, Stone), feminist economics (Folbre, Picchio, Antonopoulos, Hirway), and ecological input-output analysis (Hannon). Substantivist grounding relies on Polanyi, Sahlins, Hudson, and Gregory.

While this is just a sandbox and much more rigorous reflection would be required to use it for any substantive claims, it raises several relevant questions for theoretical reflection and potentially opens up a few novel research directions. Immediate extensions of the framework would include empirical calibration (e.g. using community currency records, time-use data, or archaeological and ethnographic evidence), extending the tool to cover important parts of Sraffa's framework that were not discussed above (e.g. joint production, fixed capital), and implementing a time dimension (for instance, by connecting to stock-flow consistent models and NK-models of interdependence). Beyond that, we believe three theoretical questions are raised by this framework: establishing more carefully the meanings of 'surplus' in a substantivist view in which the matrix is closed by the addition of social sectors; more carefully mapping the analogies and divergences between commodity-based price vectors and our measure of provisioning centrality; and operationalizing the institutional layer of the tool, which currently serves only as annotations/visualizations (some options would be: by generalizing sraffa's treatment of wages and profits to other social sectors, like taxes and reproductive care; by defining other dimensions of the matrix related to each form of integration and exploring interactions with the material-social provisioning matrix; or by applying a transaction cost framework to determine which types of intercell flows should best be understood as occuring inside organizational hierarchies within markets). Some of these themes are also addressed, from a non-material-reproduction perspective, in the author's other current work (see Ferraciolli, 2026).
