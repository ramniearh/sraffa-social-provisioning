Systemic Reproduction Parameters

Viability, Reproduction and Sectoral Balances (eigenvalues)

Calculates the dominant eigenvalue of the technical coefficients matrix. For the Economic Subsystem Eigenvalue (considered in isolation, most often an open matrix) (λ), this evaluates if the technical processes generate a physical surplus, a deficit, or exact subsistence. For the Provisioning System Eigenvalue (Extended system, most often a closed matrix) (σ), it evaluates if the entire network of socio-economic flows closes under the assumption that all outputs serve a necessary reproductive function.

Provisioning Centrality (Left Eigenvector)

Mathematical exchange proportions required to replace the inputs consumed and restart the production cycle. They act as a relational measure of the structural interdependence required for social and economic reproduction. In market economies under strict subsistence conditions, this coincides with Sraffa's exchange ratios. In other systems, it measures structural interdependence independent of the form taken by institutions.

Gross Sectoral Scale / Output (in Numéraire)

Calculates the total scale of each sector by multiplying its physical output by its relative systemic weight. This translates physically incomparable units into a single metric of scale expressed in the Numéraire.

Gross Provisioning Absorption by Social Sector

Displays (from 0% to 100%) what share of total gross flows is absorbed by social sectors versus material reproduction of the productive apparatus. This is a gross flow measure — it does not represent net surplus.

Technical Coefficients Matrix (A)

Values represent the exact physical quantity of the row sector's good required as an input to produce exactly one physical unit of the column sector's good.

Crash Course: Sraffa meets Polanyi

This interactive sandbox merges Piero Sraffa's structural matrix mathematics with Karl Polanyi's substantivist economic sociology. It provides a formalism to represent physical and social flows, shifting from a commodity-production view of the economy to a generalized model of social reproduction. Within this framework, market-exchange commodity reproduction is only a special case. The matrix maps how different sectors are integrated through Polanyi's "instituted processes" (reciprocity, redistribution, or exchange). While the matrix quantifies these flows, it does not function as a complete institutional model; rather, it highlights the underlying structural interdependence of varied social formations.

1. The Sraffian Foundation: Beyond Marginalism

Neoclassical economics generally views the economy as a marketplace of independent individuals trading goods based on subjective utility and relative scarcity. In his 1960 work Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities, Piero Sraffa proposed an alternative framework for analyzing the economy that does not rely on marginal utility or subjective demand.

Sraffa models the economy as a circular, interdependent matrix of production (as seen in our Sraffa Ch. 1 preset). To produce wheat, one needs iron (for tools); to produce iron, one needs wheat (to feed miners). This matrix allows for the calculation of systemic properties:

  • Viability (λ): Can the system physically reproduce itself, or is it operating at a physical deficit (consuming more than it produces)?
  • Relative Exchange Weights: Derived from the left eigenvector of the technical matrix, these are the mathematical exchange proportions required to replace the inputs consumed and restart the production cycle.

Sraffa also formalized the distinction between Basic Commodities (goods that enter, directly or indirectly, into the production of all other goods) and Non-Basic Commodities (goods that are produced but do not re-enter the system as productive inputs, such as luxury items). In his original book (Chapter 2), Sraffa treats the distribution of surplus (wages and profits) as external to the matrix. In our Sraffa Ch. 2 preset, we adapt this by bringing Capitalists and Workers directly into the matrix. This closes the system by assigning specific surplus shares, capturing one realized point on Sraffa's wage-profit frontier. Note that treating workers and capitalists as required matrix inputs makes a stronger structural claim than Sraffa's original formulation.

2. The Substantivist Turn: Endogenizing Social Reproduction

Sraffa’s core model focuses on the production of economic commodities, treating the distribution of the surplus (via the wage rate and profit rate) as exogenous variables determined by institutional or political struggles over monetary measures outside the mathematical system. This is not too far removed from Polanyian economic sociology, which sees economic processes are "embedded" in social relations. Every society arguably overlays a type of social and political process over what could be understood as a strictly economic process - Sraffa's example in Chapter 2 can be seen as a special case of a more general relationship between the economy and society. Feminist and ecological economics go further and argue that all social reproduction (care, community maintenance, institutional governance) is a foundational economic process.

By adding Social Sectors (Households, Communities, The State) directly into the rows and columns of the matrix, we "close the loop" and treat the reproduction of human life and culture as endogenous to the provisioning system. Sraffa's wage-profit frontier is a special case of a more general institutional problem: every society that produces a surplus must decide how to distribute it. Palace rations, potlatch obligations, and capitalist wage contracts are three institutional answers to the same structural question. The provisioning matrix makes this visible across all social formations. This allows for analytical interpretations such as:

  • Exchange values as socio-ecological metrics: When community maintenance or care work is included in the matrix, the relative value of an industrial good reflects the proportion of social reproduction structurally required to provision it.
  • Quantifying Surplus Appropriation: By tracking the flow of basic versus non-basic goods into specific social classes, the model can quantify what proportion of an economy's total output is dedicated to core provisioning versus discretionary surplus consumption.
  • Analyzing Non-Market Societies: By removing assumptions of generalized markets and money, this matrix approach can be used to analyze a hunter-gatherer foraging camp (Sahlins) or an ancient Mesopotamian palace economy (Hudson) with the same structural logic used to analyze capitalist provisioning. Importantly, the requirements of social reproduction, reflected in the broader provisioning matrix, apply to any economy, whether or not it is integrated through markets. Our 'Provisioning Centrality' can then be seen as a social-reproduction analogue to the exchange prices calculated by Sraffa. It is analytically valuable to explore the extent to which these structural weights diverge from actual market prices.

Basic vs. Non-Basic Social Flows: Sectors whose outputs are absorbed without re-entering the broader system's production are mathematically non-basic — they do not affect Provisioning Centrality scores. Luxury goods, ceremonial feasts, and financial instruments may all be non-basic in this mathematical sense, even if they are socially significant. The matrix calculation separates mathematical structural centrality from sociological importance.

3. Exploring the Preset Scenarios

These presets provide stylized sketches referencing historical and empirical literature:

  • Sahlins (Foraging): Drawing on Marshall Sahlins' concept of the "Original Affluent Society," this matrix outlines a system where subsistence foraging requires a small fraction of total labor time, leaving the majority for community reproduction and leisure. A "Feasts" sector functions as a social sink that absorbs excess biomass to generate social cohesion. Integration is modeled primarily via Reciprocity.
  • Hudson (Palace Hoard): Based on Michael Hudson's analysis of Bronze Age Mesopotamian accounting. The core domestic economy (grain, rations, corvée labor) operates through fixed administrative proportions (e.g., 60 units of grain per month of labor) via Palace Redistribution. Market Exchange is a relatively marginal activity. Hudson argues that grain rations governed domestic provisioning; silver was the long-distance accounting unit. Setting Temple as numeraire illustrates administrative weight — a grain-numeraire version would be a valid alternative formulation.
  • Capitalism: A representation of contemporary financialized, but still innovative, capitalism. F.I.R.E. (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) and Capitalists are grouped as a social class that extracts surplus value via contracts while consuming minimal physical inputs. A Technical Innovation sector transforms physical inputs into structural shifts (patents). The Biosphere is modeled as a dual-purpose sector: supplying raw materials to Industry and acting as an unpriced sink for waste, generating a structural deficit. Unpaid domestic and reproductive care work is accounted for within the Workers' sector through internal Reciprocity flows.

Note that the sandbox matrix is silent about the institutional form taken by these flows unless explicitly mapped. Nothing in the mathematics itself dictates whether these exchanges are mediated by money, state authority, or cultural obligation. This code provides a formalism to represent physical and social flows—not a complete model of institutions. It serves as a baseline for future institutional work, mapping how different areas of the matrix are handled by Polanyi's "instituted processes" (such as reciprocity, redistribution, or market exchange).
