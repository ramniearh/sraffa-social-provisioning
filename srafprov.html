System Reproduction Parameters

Viability, Reproduction and Sectoral Balances

λ is the dominant eigenvalue of the economic sectors considered in isolation. When λ < 1, the core economy produces a physical surplus; when λ = 1, it is exactly at subsistence; when λ > 1, it is in deficit. σ is the dominant eigenvalue of the full provisioning system, including social sectors. Under the closed provisioning assumption (all outputs serve a reproductive function), σ should equal 1; deviations indicate unallocated flows in the matrix.

Provisioning Centrality (Left Eigenvector)

The left eigenvector of the technical coefficients matrix A, normalized to the chosen numéraire. Each value gives the relative structural weight of that sector in sustaining overall systemic reproduction. In a market economy at subsistence, these weights coincide with Sraffa's exchange ratios. In surplus economies and non-market systems, they measure structural interdependence independently of how flows are institutionally organized.

Crash Course: Sraffa meets Polanyi

This tool applies Sraffa's input-output matrix formalism to a broader class of economies than the capitalist commodity system Sraffa analyzed. Any society that reproduces itself over time — through markets, palace redistribution, or generalized reciprocity — must sustain a set of interdependent material flows. These flows can be represented as a provisioning matrix, whose mathematical properties (eigenvalues, eigenvectors) describe structural conditions of viability regardless of the institutional form through which flows are organized. Polanyi's three forms of economic integration — market exchange, redistribution, and reciprocity — serve here as qualitative annotations identifying how each flow is governed. They have no effect on the mathematics, but prepare the ground for future institutional modeling.

1. The Sraffian Foundation: Beyond Marginalism

Neoclassical economics generally views the economy as a marketplace of independent individuals trading goods based on subjective utility and relative scarcity. In his 1960 work Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities, Piero Sraffa proposed an alternative framework for analyzing the economy that does not rely on marginal utility or subjective demand.

Sraffa models the economy as a system of circular, interdependent production processes, represented as a matrix of physical flows (as seen in our Sraffa Ch. 1 preset). To produce wheat, one needs iron (for tools); to produce iron, one needs wheat (to feed miners). This matrix allows for the calculation of systemic properties:

  • Viability (λ): Can the system physically reproduce itself, or is it operating at a physical deficit (consuming more than it produces)?
  • Provisioning Centrality: The left eigenvector of the technical coefficients matrix A, normalized to a chosen numéraire. These dimensionless weights express the relative structural importance of each sector to system reproduction. In a closed market economy at subsistence, they coincide with Sraffa's exchange ratios.

Sraffa also formalized the distinction between Basic Commodities (goods that enter, directly or indirectly, into the production of all other goods) and Non-Basic Commodities (goods that are produced but do not re-enter the system as productive inputs, such as luxury items). In his original book (Chapter 2), Sraffa treats the distribution of surplus (wages and profits) as external to the matrix. In our Sraffa Ch. 2 preset, we adapt this by bringing Capitalists and Workers directly into the matrix. This closes the system by assigning specific surplus shares, capturing one realized point on Sraffa's wage-profit frontier. Note that treating workers and capitalists as required matrix inputs makes a stronger structural claim than Sraffa's original formulation, in which wages and profits are distributional parameters applied after the physical system is solved. Bringing them into the matrix fixes one specific distributional outcome — one point on Sraffa's wage-profit frontier — and treats the reproduction of labor and governance as technical requirements of production.

2. The Substantivist Turn: Endogenizing Social Reproduction

Sraffa's core model treats wages and profits as parameters determined by institutional or political struggles outside the mathematical system — not as outputs of the model itself. This is consistent with Polanyian economic sociology, which holds that economic processes are always embedded in social institutions: the wage-profit split is not a market outcome but a social settlement. Feminist and ecological economics extend this argument further, treating the reproduction of labor (care work, domestic production) and the reproduction of natural systems (ecological flows) as necessary conditions for any economic process, not as externalities to it.

By adding Social Sectors (Households, Communities, The State) directly into the rows and columns of the matrix, we "close the loop" and treat the reproduction of human life and culture as endogenous to the provisioning system. Sraffa's wage-profit frontier is a special case of a more general institutional problem: every society that produces a surplus must decide how to distribute it. Palace rations, potlatch obligations, and capitalist wage contracts are three institutional answers to the same structural question. The provisioning matrix makes this visible across all social formations. This allows for analytical interpretations such as:

  • Provisioning Centrality as a structural measure: When care work or community maintenance is included in the matrix, the Provisioning Centrality of an industrial good incorporates the indirect cost of sustaining the labor that produces it. This makes visible a cost that market prices systematically exclude.
  • Basic and non-basic flows: By distinguishing flows of basic goods (inputs to further production) from non-basic goods (absorbed without re-entering production), the matrix identifies which sectors are structurally central to reproduction and which absorb output without contributing to it. This is a structural classification, not a normative judgment.
  • Analyzing Non-Market Societies: By removing assumptions of generalized markets and money, this matrix approach can be used to analyze a hunter-gatherer foraging camp (Sahlins) or an ancient Mesopotamian palace economy (Hudson) with the same structural logic used to analyze capitalist provisioning. Importantly, the requirements of social reproduction, reflected in the broader provisioning matrix, apply to any economy, whether or not it is integrated through markets. Provisioning Centrality is a generalization of Sraffa's exchange ratios: it measures structural interdependence independent of the institutional form through which flows are organized. In market economies under subsistence conditions the two coincide; the divergence between them in other cases is itself analytically informative.

Basic vs. Non-Basic Social Flows: Sectors whose outputs are absorbed without re-entering the broader system's production are mathematically non-basic — they do not affect Provisioning Centrality scores. Luxury goods, ceremonial feasts, and financial instruments may all be non-basic in this mathematical sense, even if they are socially significant. Non-basicness is a structural classification, not a value judgment: a sector can be mathematically non-basic (its removal does not affect Provisioning Centrality) while remaining institutionally indispensable to the social formation.

3. Exploring the Preset Scenarios

These presets provide stylized sketches referencing historical and empirical literature:

  • Sahlins (Foraging): Drawing on Marshall Sahlins' concept of the "Original Affluent Society," this matrix outlines a system where subsistence foraging requires a small fraction of total labor time, leaving the majority for community reproduction and leisure. A "Feasts" sector functions as a social sink that absorbs excess biomass to generate social cohesion. Integration is modeled primarily via Reciprocity.
  • Hudson (Palace Hoard): Based on Michael Hudson's analysis of Bronze Age Mesopotamian accounting. The core domestic economy (grain, rations, corvée labor) operates through fixed administrative proportions (e.g., 60 units of grain per month of labor) via Palace Redistribution. Market Exchange is a relatively marginal activity. Hudson argues that grain rations governed domestic provisioning; silver was the long-distance accounting unit. Setting Temple as numeraire illustrates administrative weight — a grain-numeraire version would be a valid alternative formulation.
  • Capitalism: A representation of contemporary financialized, but still innovative, capitalism. F.I.R.E. (Finance, Insurance, Real Estate) and capital ownership are grouped as a social sector whose reproduction requires few direct physical inputs but which claims a share of the system's output through property rights, contracts, and financial instruments. A Technical Innovation sector absorbs physical and labor inputs; its output (modeled as patents) does not re-enter current-period flows but represents changes to future production coefficients — making it structurally non-basic in the current matrix but dynamically significant across periods. The Biosphere is modeled as an ecological sector supplying raw materials to Industry. Its row sum exceeds its total output, indicating that the system extracts from the biosphere faster than it regenerates — a structural deficit that does not appear in any market price. Unpaid domestic and reproductive care work is accounted for within the Workers' sector: only a relatively small share of workers is required to reproduce the economy, but many more are required to reproduce the workers themselves (this cell is marked as a self-loop dominated by Reciprocity).

The matrix quantifies flows but does not model the institutional mechanisms through which they are organized. Nothing in the mathematics itself determines whether a given flow is mediated by money, administrative decree, or cultural obligation — that is what the color annotations are for. The Polanyian integration labels are a descriptive layer that prepares the ground for explicit institutional modeling, in which different integration forms would generate distinct constraints on the matrix's structure.
