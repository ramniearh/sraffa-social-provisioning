Sraffian Sector Analysis
Relative Exchange Values (Left Eigenvector)

Mathematical exchange proportions required to replace the inputs consumed and restart the production cycle. They act as a relational measure of the structural interdependence required for social and economic reproduction. These are analogous to market prices, but must be interpreted more carefully in a substantivist framework*.

Gross Sectoral Scale / Output (in Numéraire)

Calculates the total scale of each sector by multiplying its physical output by its relative exchange value. This translates physically incomparable units into a single metric of scale expressed in the Numéraire. This is an analogue of economic 'value' or 'output', but must be interpreted more carefully in a substantivist framework*.

Social Appropriation Breakdown

Displays (from 0% to 100%) how aggregate output is divided between basic social provisioning, non-basic surplus consumption, and the intermediate goods required to maintain ongoing production.

Technical Coefficients Matrix (A)

Values represent the exact physical quantity of the row sector's good required as an input to produce exactly one physical unit of the column sector's good.

Crash Course: Sraffa meets Polanyi

This interactive sandbox merges two traditions in heterodox political economy and economic sociology: Piero Sraffa's structural matrix mathematics, and Karl Polanyi's Substantivist view of the economy as embedded in social institutions. We want to move from from a commodity-production view of economic systems to a generalized social reproduction view of socioeconomic systems, for which market-exchange-based commodity reproduction is only a special case and other institutional frameworks (e.g. reciprocity, redistribution) can also account for social reproduction. A single formalism should be able to capture these different social formations, all of which share an underlying structure: in substantivist terms, all are instituted process of social reproduction.

1. The Sraffian Foundation: Beyond Marginalism

Neoclassical economics generally views the economy as a marketplace of independent individuals trading goods based on subjective utility and relative scarcity. In his 1960 work Production of Commodities by Means of Commodities, Piero Sraffa proposed an alternative framework for analyzing the economy that does not rely on marginal utility or subjective demand.

Sraffa models the economy as a circular, interdependent matrix of production (as seen in our Sraffa Ch.1 preset). To produce wheat, one needs iron (for tools); to produce iron, one needs wheat (to feed miners). This matrix allows for the calculation of systemic properties:

  • Viability (λ): Can the system physically reproduce itself, or is it operating at a physical deficit (consuming more than it produces)?
  • Relative Exchange Weights: Derived from the left eigenvector of the technical matrix, these are the mathematical exchange proportions required to replace the inputs consumed and restart the production cycle.

Sraffa also formalized the distinction between Basic Commodities (goods that enter, directly or indirectly, into the production of all other goods) and Non-Basic Commodities (goods that are produced but do not re-enter the system as productive inputs, such as luxury items). In his original book (Chapter 2), Sraffa treats the distribution of surplus (wages and profits) as external to the matrix. In our Sraffa Ch.2 (Surplus) - adapted preset, we adapt this by bringing Capitalists and Workers directly into the matrix as rows and columns, turning the distribution of surplus into a visible structural flow.

2. The Substantivist Turn: Endogenizing Social Reproduction

Sraffa’s core model focuses on the production of economic commodities, treating the distribution of the surplus (via the wage rate and profit rate) as exogenous variables determined by institutional or political struggles outside the mathematical system. Polanyian economic sociology and feminist economics, however, emphasize that economic processes are "embedded" in social relations, and that social reproduction (care, community maintenance, institutional governance) is a foundational economic process.

By adding Social Sectors (Households, Communities, The State) directly into the rows and columns of the matrix, we "close the loop" and treat the reproduction of human life and culture as endogenous to the provisioning system. This allows for analytical interpretations such as:

  • Exchange values as socio-ecological metrics: When community maintenance or care work is included in the matrix, the relative value of an industrial good reflects the proportion of social reproduction structurally required to provision it.
  • Quantifying Surplus Appropriation: By tracking the flow of basic versus non-basic goods into specific social classes, the model can quantify what proportion of an economy's total output is dedicated to core provisioning versus discretionary surplus consumption.
  • Analyzing Non-Market Societies: By removing assumptions of generalized markets and money, this matrix approach can be used to analyze a hunter-gatherer foraging camp (Sahlins) or an ancient Mesopotamian palace economy (Hudson) with the same structural logic used to analyze capitalist provisioning.

Note: Items marked with an asterisk (*) in this tool are pending better substantivist interpretation. Importantly, this matrix is currently silent about the institutional form taken by these flows. Nothing in the matrix itself dictates whether these exchanges are mediated by money, state authority, or cultural obligation. This code provides a formalism to represent physical and social flows—not a complete model of the economy, and even less so a model of institutions. However, it serves as a powerful baseline for institutional work, mapping how different areas of the matrix are handled by Polanyi's "instituted processes" (such as reciprocity, redistribution, or market exchange).
